# Temperature Forecasting — ARIMA + Fourier Regression

Time series forecasting of daily near-surface air temperature (T2M) for FIPS 36061 (New York County) using an ARIMA model with Fourier exogenous regressors to capture annual seasonality.

## Problem

Forecast daily temperatures for 2019–2020 using historical observations from 2000–2018. The yearly seasonal cycle (period = 365 days) is the dominant pattern, but fitting a seasonal ARIMA with S=365 directly is computationally infeasible — the Kalman filter's state vector grows to ~365 dimensions, causing out-of-memory failures on standard hardware (including Google Colab TPU runtimes).

## Approach

Instead of SARIMAX with a 365-day seasonal order, we model the yearly cycle through **Fourier terms** (K=5 sin/cos pairs at harmonics of 365 days) passed as exogenous regressors to a non-seasonal ARIMA. This reduces the state vector to ~10 dimensions while capturing the same periodic structure.

Model selection is handled by `pmdarima.auto_arima` with stepwise search, which identified **ARIMA(1,1,2) + Fourier(K=5)** as the best specification (AIC ≈ 28073).

### Pipeline

1. **Data loading & train/val split** — 2000–2016 for training, 2017–2018 for validation
2. **Model selection** — `auto_arima` with Fourier exogenous regressors, stepwise search over p ∈ [0,3], q ∈ [0,3], d=1
3. **Validation** — Forecast 2017–2018, evaluate MSE/RMSE against actuals
4. **Full fit & forecast** — Refit on 2000–2018, forecast 2019–2020 (731 days)
5. **Diagnostics** — Ljung-Box test, residual ACF, residual distribution
6. **Visualization** — Historical + forecast plot, validation comparison, residual analysis, monthly climatology bars

## Dataset

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| `fips` | County FIPS code (36061 — New York County) |
| `date` | Daily date, 2000-01-01 to 2018-12-31 |
| `T2M` | Near-surface air temperature (°C), range −13.18 to 29.75 |

## Results & Discussion

### Selected Model

Stepwise `auto_arima` selected **ARIMA(1,1,2) + Fourier(K=5)** with AIC ≈ 28,073. The model has 14 estimated parameters: 10 Fourier regressors (x1–x10), one AR term, two MA terms, and the error variance.

### Fourier Regressors

The 10 exogenous variables are sin/cos pairs at harmonics k=1…5 of the 365-day period. Their estimated coefficients reveal a clear pattern:

- **x1, x2 (k=1, period = 365 days):** coefficients of −6.14 and −10.69, both highly significant (p ≈ 0.000). These capture the fundamental annual cycle and dominate the model — the yearly temperature swing in New York County is approximately sinusoidal with an amplitude of roughly ±12°C around the mean.
- **x5 (k=3, period ≈ 122 days):** coefficient of −0.41, significant at p = 0.002. This third harmonic picks up a mild asymmetry in the seasonal shape — the transition from winter to summer is not perfectly symmetric with the fall-to-winter transition.
- **x3, x4, x6–x10 (k=2,4,5):** coefficients close to zero, none significant at the 5% level. The yearly cycle does not need these higher-frequency corrections, confirming that New York temperature follows a near-sinusoidal annual pattern.

This result suggests that K could be reduced to 2 or 3 without meaningful loss in forecast quality, which would further simplify the model.

### ARIMA Components

The AR(1) coefficient of 0.477 indicates moderate day-to-day persistence in the deseasonalized series — today's temperature deviation from the seasonal norm carries roughly half its signal into tomorrow. The two MA terms (−0.651, −0.321) correct for short-term shocks, with the first-lag correction being strongest. Together, the ARIMA(1,1,2) structure captures the short-range autocorrelation that the Fourier terms cannot model.

### Diagnostics

- **Ljung-Box Q(1) = 0.17, p = 0.68:** no significant residual autocorrelation at lag 1, indicating the ARIMA component has adequately captured the short-term temporal dependence.
- **Heteroskedasticity H = 1.03, p = 0.47:** residual variance is approximately constant across the sample — the model does not systematically under- or over-predict in different parts of the time series.
- **Jarque-Bera = 688.60, p = 0.00:** residuals are not perfectly normal. The skewness of −0.40 indicates slightly more extreme cold deviations than warm ones, and the kurtosis of 4.42 (vs. 3.0 for a normal distribution) reflects heavier tails than expected — occasional temperature extremes (cold snaps, heat waves) that a Gaussian error model underweights. This is typical for daily temperature data and does not invalidate the point forecasts, though confidence intervals should be interpreted with some caution as they may undercover extreme events.
- **σ² = 5.37:** the residual standard deviation is approximately √5.37 ≈ 2.3°C, meaning the model's typical daily forecast error is around ±2.3°C. For daily temperature this is a reasonable level of precision.

### Forecast Behavior

The 2019–2020 forecasts reproduce the expected seasonal shape — warm summers, cold winters — with monthly averages closely tracking the historical climatology. This is largely driven by the Fourier terms, which deterministically project the yearly cycle forward. The ARIMA component adds short-term adjustments near the forecast origin (late 2018) that gradually decay, so the forecast increasingly resembles the pure seasonal curve as the horizon extends. The 95% confidence intervals widen over time, reflecting growing uncertainty further from the last observation.

### Limitations

- **Long-horizon decay:** beyond the first few weeks, the ARIMA contribution fades and the forecast is essentially the Fourier seasonal curve plus trend. For multi-year forecasts, this model converges to climatology.
- **No exogenous climate drivers:** the model uses only the temperature history. Incorporating covariates such as atmospheric pressure, humidity, or large-scale indices (e.g., NAO, ENSO) could improve accuracy.
- **Stationarity assumption:** the model assumes the seasonal pattern is fixed across the 19-year sample. Any long-term warming trend is absorbed into the differencing but not explicitly modeled, which may bias multi-year forecasts slightly low if warming is accelerating.
- **Non-normal residuals:** the heavy-tailed error distribution means the 95% confidence intervals may be slightly too narrow for extreme temperature events.

## Why Fourier Instead of Seasonal ARIMA

Seasonal ARIMA with S=365 requires a state vector of ~365 dimensions. At each of the ~6,000 time steps, the Kalman filter performs dense matrix operations on ~365×365 matrices. This causes:

- **Memory**: state covariance matrix alone is 365² × 8 bytes ≈ 1 MB per step, with intermediate allocations pushing total usage well beyond Colab's limits
- **Time**: each model fit takes 10–60+ minutes; a grid of 35 fits would run 6–35 hours

The Fourier approach reduces the state dimension to ~10 (just the ARIMA states), making each fit take seconds. The yearly periodicity is captured equally well since temperature follows a near-sinusoidal annual pattern.
